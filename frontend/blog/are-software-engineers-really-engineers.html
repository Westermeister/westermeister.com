Are Software Engineers Really Engineers?

By Westermeister
Published on July 26, 2021

TL;DR: Yes, but only if the product is tangible. Otherwise, no.

Before I give you my reasoning, I want to go over what other people think. From what I've seen, they generally fall into 3 camps:

  1. "It's about the process. If you follow good design principles, memorize all the best practices, write quality and maintainable code... stuff like that is what makes you an engineer!"
  2. "It's about the product. You can be as detailed and meticulous in the process as you want, but if you're just making a mobile app, it doesn't matter. To be an engineer, you need to work on something more complex and difficult. People who program spaceships? Those guys are engineers!"
  3. "It's about the law. You guys have it all wrong: the term 'engineer' has to be interpreted through a legal lens. You need a license. If you've got one, you're an engineer. If not, you're not. Simple as that!"

In my opinion, they all make good points. Simultaneously, they all fall short in some way.

The people in the process camp make an honorable argument: to be an engineer, you have to hold yourself to higher standards. You have to do things the "right" way. You have to be a "professional". I like it... but I'm just not sure where to draw the line. Is there some predefined checklist of "best practices" that you have to follow, ticking off boxes one by one, and then you get to call yourself an engineer?

The people in the product camp make sense. If someone coding at NASA or SpaceX called themselves an engineer, I doubt anyone would challenge them. If someone built a to-do app and called themselves an engineer, it might be a different story. Then again, this camp suffers from the same ambiguity as the previous one: where do we draw the line? At what point does a product become impressive enough to warrant the "engineering" label?

The people in the law camp are entirely correct... but only if you live in a country where the title "engineer" is regulated e.g. Canada. That's not necessarily the case for every country.

So where does that leave us? It feels like anything goes. Coder, programmer, software developer, software engineer... what shall it be?

As someone who works with software, my take is this: we should think about the general public's point of view.

When the average Joe thinks about engineering, he's thinking about tangible objects. Mechanical engineers can show the machines they've built. Electrical engineers can point to the electronics you use every day. Civil engineers can take you to the bridge or the dam that they built. Chemical engineers can produce a weird-ass glowing liquid.

...but "software engineers" often can't point to anything.

What we build is invisible. Maybe it exists in some data center. Or on the display of a smartphone. Or on a computer monitor. Our product isn't tangible. It's just some invisible process running in a box.

I think that's why this question is even a question in the first place. It just doesn't feel intuitive that some guy typing on a computer and creating something intangible should be called an "engineer".

And of course, the obvious exception is when we actually are producing something tangible. A good example would be a Roomba. When the average Joe asks you what you do for a living, you can say "I'm a software engineer" and point to something tangible that you helped create: in this case, the Roomba. Personally, I doubt he'd question your title.

Otherwise, we should call ourselves either a "coder", a "programmer", or a "software developer". All of these terms are synonymous and perfectly respectable, although I'd wager "software developer" is slightly more professional-sounding to the general public, if that matters to you.

And look: I get that a lot of people simply want to call themselves engineers. It sounds cool. It makes them feel proud. And if my coworkers referred to themselves as engineers, I would respect that, because getting along is more important than shoving opinions down each other's throats.

But as for me, I'll stick to being a developer.
